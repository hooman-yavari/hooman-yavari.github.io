Dynamic Stretching vs Static Stretching

Sports Science • 2026

Dynamic Stretching vs. Static Stretching: Which Should Athletes Use?

As an athlete, I have always been told to warm up properly before jumping into training or exercise, and stretching is often considered one of the most important parts of both warm-ups and cool-downs. However, I always wondered which type of stretching is actually better. Looking back, I’m not even sure my coaches agreed on the answer, since each seemed to have a different opinion. In this article, I will explain two main types of stretching and when athletes should use each one.

Static stretching :

I’m going to begin by explaining traditional static stretching, which is commonly used and was once considered an injury-preventing pre-exercise movement, but is not strongly supported by modern science for warm-up purposes. However, it still has value in improving flexibility. Static stretching is holding a stretch in one position without moving for around 10-30 seconds. This helps temporarily increase muscle length and reduce muscle tightness, allowing the muscle to move more comfortably through its full range of motion. A few examples include: Upper back stretch, Shoulder stretch, Hamstring stretch, Calf stretch, quadriceps stretch, etc.

Dynamic stretching :

As research on warm-up strategies has evolved, dynamic stretching has become increasingly recommended before exercise and athletic activities, mainly because it encourages your muscles and joints to move through their full range of motion. Dynamic stretching involves moving your muscles and joints through a full range of motion without holding a position. Instead of staying still in a stretch, you stay active by swinging one leg forward and back in a controlled motion for several reps. Some other examples are: hip circles, walking lunges, high knees, jumping jacks, etc.

Dynamic stretch or Static stretch?

Returning to the original question, which type should athletes use? The answer depends on the sport, the context, and the timing of the activity. Dynamic stretches are better used during warm-ups because they prepare the body for movement by increasing blood flow and mobility, whereas static stretching can briefly reduce explosive strength. This is because dynamic movements activate the nervous system and mimic the actions used in sport, helping the body prepare more effectively for performance. In a study involving 24 healthy individuals, researchers found that dynamic stretching improved flexibility and reduced muscle stiffness. The benefits were observed immediately after stretching and lasted for up to 90 minutes. This suggests that dynamic stretches are a really good choice for warming up due to the long-lasting effect. Professional athletes like Soccer players, Basketball players, Footballers, Tennis players, Runners, and volleyball players can benefit the most from dynamic stretches because their sport involves speed, power, and fast changes of movement. It is still a good idea to use static stretches during warm-up for some sports like Gymnastics, Martial arts, Body building, Ballet / Dancing, but usually still combined with dynamic work for the best outcome.

When to use Static Stretching?

Now I know I mentioned the uses of dynamic stretching so many times, but it doesn’t mean that you won't need static stretching anymore! Static stretching is best used when you finish your workout, and the cool-down phase begins. After training, the body is already warm, so static stretching is used to: help reduce muscle tightness gradually bring the heart rate down improve flexibility over time Relax the muscles after activity

Conclusion:

Overall, both static and dynamic stretching have their place in training, but they are used for different purposes and at different times. Dynamic stretching is generally more suitable before exercise because it prepares the body for movement, improves mobility, and helps the muscles activate in a way that matches sporting actions. Static stretching, on the other hand, is more effective after exercise during the cool-down phase, where it helps reduce muscle tightness and support flexibility over time. Understanding when to use each type of stretching can help athletes improve performance and train more effectively while reducing unnecessary risk of poor preparation.
